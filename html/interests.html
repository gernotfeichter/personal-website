Note: I decided to use the payback period as the most important indicator to compare energy production systems below.

Solar

Solar Photovoltaic is a viable energy production technology and has a payback period of six to ten years [1].

Wind

Things are little bit more complicated with wind energy, it seems to be more economical in the large scale [5] than in the small scale at the moment [4]. It is my hope that this could change in future, see the following section on Betz's law below, that I am currently evaluating.

Betz's law

Graham Flowers posted a video on youtube [3] in which he claims that his wind turbine is more efficient than what the conventional model of Betz allows (more than 59% efficiency).

I considered it worthwhile to evaluate the proposed solution!


Claim: Wind turbine with corrugated foils with a diameter of 2.9 m produces 794 watts at a wind speed of 6.7 m/s.

Calculation of Betz's limit with standard air with 0% humid air at 0 °C

Using what wolfram alpha considers "air", I obtained the following results:

How much volume of air passes through the turbine in one second? 44 m^3.
https://www.wolframalpha.com/input?i=volume+of+a+cylinder+with+2.9m+diameter+and+6.7m+hight

https://www.wolframalpha.com/input?i=44m%5E3+air

What is the kinetic energy of 56.1 kg moving at 6.7 m/s? 0.3498 watt hours
https://www.wolframalpha.com/input?i=energy+in+56.1kg+moving+at+6.7m%2Fs

What could have gone wrong here? I think that the most likely reasons for my result being slightly off to Graham's is that we performed the calculations with different air temperature or humidity levels. For sake of simplicity I will therefore fix the temperature to a relatively cold value of 15 °C and repeat the rest of the procedure at 0 % and 100% humidity levels.

Storage

The importance of a storage arises from the fluctuating nature of wind and solar. Unfortunately, there are no cost-effective energy storage solutions available at the moment [2]. Fortunately, there are many promising technologies on the rise, though too many to list.

Conclusions

It is currently not financially viable to become self-sustaining by going 100% renewable, but it is possible from a technical perspective! The current best bet is to go solar and possibly combine it with a heat pump to partially live on renewables. Advancements in storage solutions are required, advancements in energy production solutions could furthermore enhance economic viability.
