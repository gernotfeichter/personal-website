Introduction

During my studies in Information Management I came across the P vs NP problem, which was introduced to us students by our professor in Informatics as being a major unsolved problem in the field. During the preparation for one of the exams I found out that a prize money of $1 million would be offered for a proper solution by the Clay Mathematics Institute, which caught my attention.

Furthermore, I found the problem itself was quite easy to be understood and did not even require too much previous knowledge, which I did not already possess. As I had always enjoyed thinking about various issues of logical nature this was definitely an issue I was willing to spend some effort on. Being convinced I had always been quite talented in truly understanding things gave me the confidence I could eventually solve the problem.

Being unaware of how long it would take me to come up with a plausible resolution, I must have spent at least a year of total spare time thinking about the problem, distributed over a longer period of time. Initially, I thought that it would only require me to have a certain amount of shrewdness and that the time aspect would be negligible, which turned out to be far from reality. It took me a while to truly get to the core of the problem, learning from each previous failure and finally coming up with what I regard as the proof. Then I put the solution down on paper in a structured manner, making it as easy as possible for the reader to understand.

Referee: 1

Comments to the Author
The paper claims to present a proof that P \ne NP by claiming a lower bound of 2^(n/2) for the subset sum problem.
However, the argument is, at best, missing many ideas to be a true lower bound proof.

The paper spends a long time arguing that in order to determine a 'no' answer to subset sum one must guarantee that each of the 2^n possible subsets is either too large or too small and that in the worst case one can eliminate at most 2^(n/2) of these subsets. However, even if one can 'only' eliminate 2^(n/2) subsets from explicit consideration does not imply that one need to enumerate all remaining subsets to determine there is no solution. For example, in solving the minimum spanning tree problem, one needn't enumerate an exponential number of feasible solutions to prove that the solution you find is the best solution.

Comments:

Dear author,

It is our policy that such a submission claiming to resolve the P versus NP problem (and or its related
big open problems) need to provide the explanation why the proposed technique can overcome several
proof theoretic barriers. For example, we can consider a relativized world where P = NP; such a world
has a combinatrial problem corresponding to the subset sum problem. That is, the problem corresponding
to the subset sum problem is indeed in P in such a relativized world. It is the author's responsibility
why the author's argument is still consistent with such a world. Unfortunately, I have not been able
to find such an explanation in this submission; therefore, I would like to report the rejection of
this submission to the editorial office.

Sincerely,
Osamu Watanabe
